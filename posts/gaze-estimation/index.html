DD: Gaze Estimation

Nov 15, 2024

Gaze estimation is the process of determining where an individual is looking at. This is a surprisingly active research area, because human eye gaze is valuable data. Cheng et al. (2024)1 describes five types of gaze estimation methods.

The earliest approaches involved attaching sensors like electrodes onto the skin near the eye.

Originally I got the dataset here, but it turns out the MPIIFaceGaze dataset and the MPIIGaze dataset are different.

I iterated a few approaches.

My first attempt involved roughly replicating the model and results from Zhang et al. (2015)2. After obtaining the MPIIGaze dataset presented in the paper, I had to conduct many annoying preprocessing steps before I could even start training the model. The dataset consists of 15 individual's and calibration info such as camera intrinsics for each.

  • I used Google's MediaPipe for face landmarking, departing from whatever weird method Zhang et al. used. It would likely throw of the model if I used their face landmarks for training then MediaPipe for inference, so I reprocessed each sample in the dataset with MediaPipe and stored the landmarks in a pickle file, for use in training.
  • The dataset provides camera intrinsics for each participant's webcam. I threw OpenCV's PNP solver those intrinsics paired with a few face landmarks and it spits out an estimate of the 3d head orientation. I can't believe it worked with any degree of accuracy, because from what I can tell MediaPipe just bullshits the z-values for the face landmarks.
  • I wrote a function to crop out the eye image given the face landmarks. It then resizes the image to 60x36, greyscales it, and histogram-equalizes the pixel values.

After that, I implemented the CNN described in the paper using PyTorch. The results even with my first naive approach was very encouragingâ€”it was exhilarating to see my cursor respond to eye movements, albeit very roughly, during the initial successful run.

I deviated from their method by not concating the 2d head angle vector to the fully connected layer, as I found it had no impact on the loss. In addition, I applied the CNN separately to both eyes and took the mean of the predictions as the final predicted 2d gaze angle.

Finally, changing the fully connected layer to 64 rather than 500 decreases the test loss and makes the model emprically more accurate for my demo for some reason.

I should mention, I found my own camera intrinsics by following this camera calibration guide. It was very easyâ€”I just had to take a couple pictures from my webcam of me holding my iPad with a checkboard pattern all over the place.
